There is a particular kind of fear that visits every generation — the dread that this time, the machine has gone too far. That this new invention does not merely change the way people work, but erases the premise of work itself. It arrived when the power loom appeared in Lancashire. It arrived again when the assembly line swallowed Detroit's craft shops. It surfaced once more when the desktop computer began clearing out typing pools and accountancy departments. And here it is again, wearing the face of a language model.

The fear is not irrational. But it is, historically speaking, incomplete. The record of the past two and a half centuries tells a different story — one of disruption that is real, painful for many, and ultimately generative. The question worth asking is not whether AI will change everything. It already has. The question is whether we have learned anything from the five times this happened before.

I / 1760–1850

When machines challenged human hands

The first factory workers of the Industrial Revolution did not experience technological progress as an abstraction. They experienced it as a threat in the shape of a spinning jenny. Skilled handloom weavers who had spent years mastering their craft found, seemingly overnight, that a machine tended by an unskilled child could outproduce them by a factor of eight.

Their response was direct. Between 1811 and 1816, bands of workers calling themselves Luddites smashed machinery across the English Midlands. They were not opposed to technology in principle. They were opposed to poverty, which the machines were delivering faster than the economy could absorb. Parliament, alarmed, made machine-breaking a capital offence.

"The introduction of the power loom and of steam power into manufacturing has so completely superseded the greater part of what previously existed that those who were engaged in it are thrown out of employment."

— Parliamentary Committee Report, 1834

What the Parliamentary committee did not foresee — could not have foreseen — was the scale of what was coming. The same industrial machinery that destroyed handloom weaving created factory management, mechanical engineering, railway construction, and a completely new profession called industrial inspector. The net employment effect of industrialization was not subtraction but multiplication.

This tension between aggregate benefit and individual cost will recur in every revolution that follows.

II / 1870–1920

Electricity and the concentration of power

The second disruption arrived more quietly. Electricity did not arrive with the dramatic imagery of a smoking factory chimney. It crept in through the wires, and the fear it generated was less about joblessness than about something more abstract: monopoly.

When Edison and Westinghouse fought the "War of Currents" in the 1880s and 1890s, the public was watching not just a technical dispute but a contest over who would control the infrastructure of modern civilization. The fear was that a single corporation — Edison's, or Westinghouse's, or later Morgan's — would own the wires and charge whatever they liked.

The assembly line compounded this anxiety. Henry Ford did not invent mass production, but he perfected it in a way that made its human costs visible. The worker on Ford's line in Highland Park did not make a car. He tightened a bolt. All day. The same bolt. The fear was not unemployment but something subtler: the hollowing out of craft, the replacement of a skilled human with a precise but replaceable motion.

Ford's $5 day — introduced in 1914, twice the going wage — was not charity. It was a solution to a problem that AI companies will likely face too: how do you sell the products your machines make if the people who might buy them can't afford them? The electrification era invented a new equilibrium not through philanthropy but through economic necessity.

III / 1940–1985

The computer threatens the office

For the first time in the history of technological disruption, the 1950s produced a machine that threatened not backs but minds. The computer's first target was the human computer — the rooms full of mathematicians, mostly women, who spent their working days performing calculations by hand. They were replaced first in aerospace, then in banking, then everywhere.

The white-collar fear was distinct from anything that had come before. Factory workers had always known their labor was physical and therefore, in principle, mechanical. But clerks, accountants, and data-entry operators had believed themselves safe in the realm of thought. The computer's arrival was a category violation — it suggested that systematic mental work was no more sacred than systematic physical work.

"The computer will be able to do whatever the human mind can do." The prediction was made in 1958 by Herbert Simon and Allen Newell. They were not wrong about the destination, only about the timetable — by about sixty-five years.

What actually happened: clerical work did not disappear. It transformed. The computer created demand for computer operators, systems analysts, and a new profession that barely existed in 1950 — software engineer. By the mid-1980s, more Americans worked in computing-related fields than had worked in all of the industries the computer was said to be destroying.

IV / 1990–2007

The internet kills everything

The Internet arrived with a particular kind of hubris. Its early evangelists did not merely promise efficiency; they promised the end of geography, the end of scarcity, the end of middlemen, and, in some quarters, the end of the nation-state. The reaction from incumbents — travel agents, record labels, classified advertising, newspaper publishers — was precisely the kind of fear you would expect from people watching their business models dissolve in real time.

The dot-com boom and bust of 1999–2001 is often misread as a story about irrational exuberance. It was that, but it was also a story about timing. Almost everything the dot-com investors predicted came true. They were simply wrong by about a decade. Amazon, Google, and Facebook did eventually do what Webvan, Pets.com, and Kozmo promised. The vision was correct; the schedule was not.

What is less remembered is the exceptional creativity of the jobs the Internet created. The role of "search engine optimisation specialist" did not exist in 1995. Neither did podcast producer, social media manager, UX researcher, or cloud architect. These are not marginal niche occupations — collectively they employ tens of millions of people worldwide, most of them paid more than the jobs the Internet displaced.

V / 2007–2022

The smartphone and the attention economy

The iPhone's introduction in 2007 is often narrated as a product story. It was actually an architectural one. The smartphone did not merely create a new device category; it shifted the fundamental site of economic value from hardware and software to something that had not previously been understood as a commodity — human attention.

This was qualitatively different from previous disruptions. Earlier revolutions had displaced workers or businesses. The mobile revolution displaced something harder to quantify: time. The average smartphone user now spends more than four hours per day on their device. That time had to come from somewhere — and it came, largely, from television, newspapers, physical retail browsing, and unsupervised boredom.

"We always overestimate the change that will occur in the next two years and underestimate the change that will occur in the next ten."

— Bill Gates, "The Road Ahead," 1995

The mobile era also produced the first disruption where the product was free and the user was the product. This generated a new category of fear — not the loss of jobs but the loss of privacy, of autonomy, of the ability to be bored. These concerns were legitimate, and largely unresolved.

VI / 2020–Present

AI automates cognition itself

⚠ Fastest transition on record

Every previous disruption automated something we could, in retrospect, describe as mechanical — even if it didn't feel that way at the time. Factories automated muscle. Computers automated calculation. The Internet automated distribution. Each time, the response was: "but surely it cannot touch creativity, judgment, empathy, language."

The large language model is a direct challenge to that refuge. It does not merely perform tasks faster than humans; it performs tasks that were previously defined as the exclusive province of human intelligence: writing, argument, analysis, code generation, legal reasoning, medical diagnosis support. The scope is categorically different from anything before it.

There is also a crucial asymmetry that has no precedent in the previous five revolutions. Every earlier technology had a long ramp — decades during which society could observe it, regulate it, and adapt. The steam engine took sixty years to become economically dominant. The personal computer took thirty. The Internet took twenty. ChatGPT reached 100 million users in two months.

The counterargument — and there is one — is the same argument that was available in 1811, 1895, 1955, 1999, and 2007: every previous technology that appeared to threaten work ended up creating more of it than it destroyed. The tasks that disappeared were replaced by tasks that could not have been imagined before the technology existed. There is no reason, in principle, why AI should be different.

There is, however, one reason to be less certain than in previous eras. When the power loom replaced handloom weavers, the weavers were replaced by machine operators, mechanics, and factory managers — humans who could do things machines could not. When AI replaces a knowledge worker, the question is: what is left that AI cannot eventually do? The historical pattern holds only if there remains a category of irreducibly human work.

What the pattern suggests

Speed. Adoption curves measured in months, not decades. Society's adaptive mechanisms were built for slower change.

New jobs will emerge. They always have. But the lag between displacement and creation may be longer than anything previously experienced.

Scope. Previous revolutions automated specific categories. AI targets the cognitive layer that sits beneath all professional work.

Hybrid roles. The highest-value workers in every previous revolution were those who mastered the new tool, not those who competed with it.

Concentration. Building frontier AI requires capital at a scale that makes early computing look affordable. The few who can play will dominate.

Regulation follows. Every powerful technology eventually attracted governance. AI is early in that cycle — not exempt from it.

The pattern behind every technological revolution

The lesson history teaches — and doesn't

There is a version of the "AI is not the first disruptor" argument that is offered as reassurance: everything worked out before, so it will work out again. This reading is too comfortable. History is not a guarantee. It is a dataset — and every data point came with real casualties.

The Luddite frame has become a term of mockery, used to dismiss anyone who questions technological change. This is a disservice to the historical Luddites, who were not opponents of technology but advocates for workers being crushed by a transition they had no power to shape. They were wrong about the long run and right about the immediate present — a distinction worth making more carefully than we usually do.

What history actually teaches is three things. First: the fears are almost always exaggerated in the short run. Second: the disruption is almost always underestimated in the long run. Third — and this is the part that gets left out — the distribution of benefit from technological change does not happen automatically. It is contested, political, and deliberate.

The Industrial Revolution did not produce its best outcomes because markets were left alone. It produced them because reformers fought for factory legislation, universal education, and labor rights that put more of the productivity gains into the hands of workers. That battle was not won by the technology. It was won by the people.

AI may well follow the historical pattern. The new industries may dwarf the displaced ones. The productivity gains may be distributed widely enough to constitute progress. But none of that is determined by the technology itself. It is determined by what happens next — in policy, in labor markets, in corporate governance, in how we choose to answer the question that every technological revolution eventually forces: who does this serve, and at whose expense?

AI is not the first disruptor. But it may be moving fast enough that the usual period of gradual adaptation — the two or three decades during which societies absorb the shock — is no longer available. If that is true, then the most important thing history tells us is not that everything turned out fine. It is that everything turned out fine because people made it so, against considerable resistance, across considerable time.

The question is whether we have the time, and the will, to do it again.
